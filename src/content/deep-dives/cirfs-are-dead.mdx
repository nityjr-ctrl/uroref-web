## What Actually Changed

The AUA 2026 Surgical Stone Guidelines have removed "Clinically Insignificant Residual Fragments" — CIRFs — as a valid reporting category for post-operative stone outcomes.

The term is gone. Not quietly updated. Gone.

The reasoning is straightforward: calling a fragment "clinically insignificant" is a clinical judgement that was increasingly being made on the basis of size alone, without accounting for location, composition, or the individual patient's risk profile. The guidelines now require residual stone burden to be reported accurately and objectively — size, location, and anatomical context — so that the person making the clinical decision actually has the information they need.

## Why the Old Term Was Always a Bit Off

The phrase "clinically insignificant" carries an implicit message: this fragment does not need to be acted on. But a 4mm fragment in the lower pole of a single kidney is a different clinical problem from a 4mm fragment in a normal kidney with no metabolic risk factors, even if both technically qualified as "CIRFs" under the old framing.

The label encouraged a kind of shorthand that skipped the thinking. And in stone disease — where recurrence rates are high, composition matters, and some patients are high-risk — that shorthand was starting to cause problems.

It was not that the concept was invented in bad faith. It was genuinely useful as a quick way to say "this is not the thing we need to urgently deal with." But it drifted into a category that felt more definitive than it actually was.

## What to Say Instead

This is the practical bit. Next time you are presenting a post-op KUB on the ward round:

**Do not say:** *"There's a CIRF."*

**Do say:** *"There's a 3mm fragment in the lower pole calyx. No hydronephrosis. Plan to discuss at stone MDT / at follow-up / with Mr [Consultant] re: surveillance vs. further intervention."*

That is it. Three things:

1. **Size** — give the measurement in millimetres
2. **Location** — lower pole, ureterovesical junction, proximal ureter — be specific
3. **Context** — does it change the immediate plan? Is there a follow-up in place?

You are not being asked to make the decision. You are being asked to give the consultant the information they need to make it. That is all.

## A Small Shift With Real Consequences

Retiring a term from a guideline document sounds like a minor administrative update. In practice, it reflects something more meaningful — a push towards more honest, precise communication about what surgery actually achieved, rather than what we hoped it achieved.

For a trainee, the adjustment is not dramatic. It is mostly about building a habit: describe what you see, quantify it, and let the information do the work rather than reaching for a label that does the thinking for you.

The consultants have always known the fragment is there. The question they are asking when you present the post-op imaging is whether you understand what it means.
