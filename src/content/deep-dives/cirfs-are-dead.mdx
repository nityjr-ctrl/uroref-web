## What Actually Changed

The AUA 2026 Surgical Stone Guidelines have removed "Clinically Insignificant Residual Fragments" (CIRFs) as a valid reporting category for post-operative stone outcomes.

The term is gone. Not quietly updated. Gone.

The reasoning is pretty straightforward. Calling a fragment "clinically insignificant" is a clinical judgement, but it was increasingly being made on size alone, without thinking about location, composition, or the individual patient's risk profile. The guidelines now want residual stone burden reported accurately and specifically, so the person making the actual decision has the information they need.

## Why the Old Term Was Always a Bit Off

The phrase "clinically insignificant" carries an implicit message: don't worry about this, nothing needs doing. But a 4mm fragment in the lower pole of a single kidney is a completely different problem from a 4mm fragment in a normal kidney with no metabolic risk factors, even if both technically qualified as CIRFs under the old framing.

The label encouraged a kind of shorthand that skipped the thinking. In stone disease, where recurrence rates are high, composition matters, and some patients are genuinely high-risk, that shorthand was starting to cause real problems.

It wasn't invented in bad faith. It was genuinely useful as a quick way to say "this isn't the urgent thing right now." But it drifted into something that felt more definitive than it actually was.

## What to Say Instead

This is the practical bit. Next time you're presenting a post-op KUB on the ward round:

**Don't say:** *"There's a CIRF."*

**Do say:** *"There's a 3mm fragment in the lower pole calyx. No hydronephrosis. Plan to discuss at stone MDT / at follow-up / with the consultant re: surveillance vs. further intervention."*

That's it. Three things:

1. **Size** - the measurement in millimetres
2. **Location** - lower pole, ureterovesical junction, proximal ureter - be specific
3. **Context** - does it change the immediate plan? Is there a follow-up already in place?

You're not being asked to make the decision. You're being asked to give the consultant the information they need to make it. That's all.

## A Small Shift With Real Consequences

Retiring a term from a guideline document sounds like a minor admin update. In practice it reflects something more meaningful: a push towards honest, precise communication about what surgery actually achieved, rather than what we hoped it achieved.

For a trainee the adjustment isn't dramatic. It's mostly about building one habit: describe what you see, quantify it, and let the information do the work rather than reaching for a label that does the thinking for you.

The consultants have always known the fragment is there. The question they're asking when you present the post-op imaging is whether you understand what it means.
