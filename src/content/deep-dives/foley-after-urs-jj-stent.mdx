Post-ureteroscopy management is one of those areas where habit drives practice more than evidence. Stent insertion after URS is nearly universal for most cases. But the Foley? It often goes in at the end of the case without much discussion. A 2025 BJUI RCT from the EULIS-YAU collaboration decided to actually test whether that's right.

## What the Trial Did

Single-centre RCT, 112 patients (56 per arm). Adults 18 to 65, kidney or proximal ureteric stones under 2cm, negative urine culture. All had flexible URS plus a 4.8Fr JJ stent with a ureteric access sheath and dusting lithotripsy technique.

Group A: no Foley post-op. Group B: Foley placed at the end of the procedure.

Primary outcomes were IPSS scores, stent symptom scores (USSQ), and inflammatory markers (WBC, CRP, PCT, creatinine) at follow-up. Secondary outcome: complications at 30 days.

## What It Found

No significant difference in IPSS, inflammatory markers, or biochemistry between the two groups.

Complications were 5.4% in the no-Foley arm versus 8.9% in the Foley arm. All grade I or II on Clavien-Dindo. The Foley group trended slightly worse, though not statistically significant.

The one finding that went the other way: USSQ total body pain subscore was lower in the Foley group, suggesting the catheter may reduce early discomfort from bladder fullness or detrusor spasm in the immediate post-op period.

## What This Means in Practice

The conclusion from the authors is clear: no biochemical or functional advantage to routine Foley placement. Some patients may benefit from short-term catheterisation for analgesia, but as a blanket policy the evidence doesn't support it.

The EULIS-YAU provenance matters. This isn't a single-centre curiosity piece. It's a collaborative EAU section trial, which gives the finding more weight than most single-surgeon series.

The body pain signal is worth keeping in mind. Patients with pre-existing bladder sensitivity, overactive bladder, or significant haematuria at end of case might still benefit from overnight drainage. That's a clinical judgement call. Routine placement without that reasoning? Less justifiable now.
