The prevailing assumption for decades has been that testosterone drives prostate cancer, so low T at diagnosis must be protective, or at worst irrelevant. Two 2025 papers challenge this directly, and the data are hard to dismiss.

## The Studies

**BJUI Compass (2025):** retrospective cohort, 2,544 prostate cancer patients from a Finnish national registry. Testosterone groups defined as low (below 8.0 nmol/L), grey zone (8.0 to 10.4 nmol/L), and normal (above 10.4 nmol/L).

Low T at diagnosis was an independent predictor of worse overall survival: univariable HR 1.67 (95% CI 1.37 to 2.05), multivariable HR 1.58 (95% CI 1.24 to 1.98), adjusted for age, PSA, Gleason, T and N stage, Charlson comorbidity index, and diabetes. The effect was consistent across age groups under and over 70. No survival difference in the grey zone versus normal T.

**Cancer Medicine (2025):** PSA-screened cohort, 350 patients. Low T at diagnosis was associated with more advanced T-stage, higher prostate cancer-specific mortality (AHR 2.70, p=0.01 in patients with no or minimal comorbidity), and higher all-cause mortality (AHR 1.90, p=0.02).

## Why This Is More Than a Confounding Problem

The obvious counter: low T is a marker of frailty and metabolic dysfunction, so the worse outcomes reflect underlying health, not the testosterone itself. That explanation is partially addressed by the Finnish study's multivariable model, which adjusted for Charlson comorbidity index. The association held.

The more interesting mechanism: androgen-deprived tumour microenvironments may select for castration-resistant phenotypes earlier. Cancer that grows in a low-T environment may already be adapted to survive without androgen stimulation. That makes it harder to treat with ADT when it's eventually needed.

Neither study proves causation. Neither addresses whether treating the hypogonadism changes outcomes. But a 2,544-patient cohort with consistent hazard ratios across age subgroups is more than a hypothesis.

## What to Do With This Clinically

We already check PSA, Gleason, stage, and increasingly PSMA PET before deciding on treatment intent. Should testosterone be routinely measured at diagnosis too?

These data suggest yes, not to treat the hypogonadism immediately, but as a prognostic variable that might inform surveillance versus treatment thresholds and how you counsel patients about their likely trajectory.

It's also a useful corrective to the "testosterone causes prostate cancer" narrative. Low T isn't safe territory. It may just mean the cancer is already running on a different fuel.
