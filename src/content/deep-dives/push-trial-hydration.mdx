I've said something like that more times than I can count. It's the default. It's easy to give, easy for patients to understand. And it's not wrong - increased fluid intake is associated with reduced stone recurrence in the observational data, and it remains standard first-line guidance.

But a large, well-run randomised controlled trial published in the Lancet in March 2026 has just made that advice considerably more complicated.

## What the PUSH Trial Did

The PUSH trial followed 1,658 symptomatic kidney stone patients over two years, randomising them to either an intensive behavioural hydration intervention or standard care. The intervention worked. Participants successfully increased their urine output. Urine volumes went up. The surrogate endpoint was achieved.

Stone recurrence didn't budge.

The primary outcome (symptomatic stone events) showed a hazard ratio of 0.96 (95% CI 0.77 to 1.20). That's essentially no effect. Increasing urine volume, through a well-designed and well-supported intervention, didn't meaningfully reduce stone recurrence in this general cohort.

## Why This Matters

The PUSH trial is important because it separates two things the existing literature had blurred together: the surrogate endpoint of urine volume, and the hard clinical endpoint of actual stone events.

For years the reasoning has been: more water, more dilute urine, lower supersaturation, fewer stones. That's physiologically sensible, and observational data supported it. But PUSH is asking a different question: does actually achieving higher urine output in real patients change the clinical outcome?

In a general stone-forming cohort, the answer appears to be: not reliably.

## What This Doesn't Mean

It doesn't mean hydration advice is useless and should be abandoned. That would be overcorrecting from one trial.

Low urine volume remains a modifiable risk factor. Patients who are genuinely chronically dehydrated probably do benefit from increasing intake. What PUSH tells us is that hydration alone, as a standalone strategy for an unselected stone-forming population, isn't sufficient to meaningfully shift recurrence rates at a population level.

The honest clinical implication is narrower: we should stop presenting "drink more water" as a prevention strategy in its own right, and start treating it as one component of a broader metabolic assessment.

## The Shift in Practice

The practical takeaway for a trainee is about what to do in clinic rather than what to tell patients.

For any patient presenting with recurrent stone disease, the question isn't just "how much are they drinking" anymore. It's what's driving the stone formation metabolically. That means:

- **24-hour urine profiling** for recurrent stone formers - calcium, oxalate, citrate, uric acid, volume, pH
- **Stone composition analysis** if a stone has been retrieved
- **Serum calcium** to exclude primary hyperparathyroidism
- **Targeted dietary and pharmacological advice** based on the actual metabolic abnormality, not a generic hydration prescription

None of this is new guidance. It's been in the EAU and AUA guidelines for years. But PUSH gives us a clearer reason to take it seriously rather than defaulting to the easier conversation.

## On the Ward Round and in Clinic

If you've got a stone patient in front of you - post-PCNL, post-URS, or presenting with recurrence - the PUSH trial shifts the default from "make sure they're drinking enough" to "this patient needs a metabolic workup to understand why they're forming stones."

That's a slightly harder conversation. It's also the more honest one.

The water advice still goes in. It just can't be the whole plan anymore.
