I have said something like that more times than I can count. It is the default. It is easy to give as advice. It is easy for patients to understand. And it is not wrong — increased fluid intake is associated with reduced stone recurrence in the epidemiological literature, and it remains standard first-line guidance.

But a large, well-conducted randomised controlled trial published in the Lancet in March 2026 has just made that advice considerably more complicated.

## What the PUSH Trial Did

The PUSH trial followed 1,658 symptomatic kidney stone patients over two years, randomising them to either an intensive behavioural hydration intervention or standard care. The intervention worked — participants successfully increased their urine output. Urine volumes went up. The surrogate endpoint was achieved.

Stone recurrence did not budge.

The primary outcome — symptomatic stone events — showed a hazard ratio of 0.96 (95% CI 0.77–1.20). That is essentially no effect. Increasing urine volume, through a well-designed and adherence-supported intervention, did not meaningfully reduce the rate of symptomatic stone recurrence in this general cohort of stone formers.

## Why This Matters

The PUSH trial is methodologically important because it separates two things that the existing literature had blurred together: the surrogate endpoint of urine volume and the hard clinical endpoint of stone events.

For years, the reasoning has been: more water → more dilute urine → lower supersaturation → fewer stones. That is physiologically sensible, and observational data supported it. But PUSH is asking a different question: does actually achieving higher urine output — in real patients, in a real intervention — change the clinical outcome?

The answer, in a general stone-forming cohort, appears to be: not reliably.

## What This Does Not Mean

It does not mean hydration advice is useless and should be abandoned. That would be overcorrecting from one trial.

Low urine volume remains a modifiable risk factor. Patients who are genuinely chronically dehydrated probably do benefit from increasing intake. The PUSH trial tells us that hydration alone — as a standalone strategy for an unselected stone-forming population — is not sufficient to meaningfully shift recurrence rates at the population level.

The honest clinical implication is narrower: we should stop presenting "drink more water" as a prevention strategy in its own right, and start treating it as one component of a broader metabolic assessment.

## The Shift in Practice

For a trainee, the practical takeaway is about what to do in clinic rather than what to tell patients.

For any patient presenting with recurrent stone disease, the question is no longer just "how much are they drinking" — it is what is driving the stone formation metabolically. That means:

- **24-hour urine profiling** for recurrent stone formers — calcium, oxalate, citrate, uric acid, volume, pH
- **Stone composition analysis** if a stone has been retrieved
- **Serum calcium** to exclude primary hyperparathyroidism
- **Targeted dietary and pharmacological advice** based on the actual metabolic abnormality, not a generic hydration prescription

None of this is new guidance. It has been in the EAU and AUA guidelines for years. But PUSH gives us a clearer reason to take it seriously rather than defaulting to the easier conversation.

## On the Ward Round and in Clinic

If you have a stone patient in front of you — post-PCNL, post-URS, or presenting with recurrence — the PUSH trial changes the default recommendation from "make sure they're drinking enough" to "this patient needs a metabolic workup to understand why they are forming stones."

That is a slightly harder conversation. It also happens to be the more honest one.

The water advice still goes in. It just should not be the whole plan.
