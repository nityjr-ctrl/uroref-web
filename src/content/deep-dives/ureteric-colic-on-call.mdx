Ureteric colic is one of the most common urological presentations on call. Getting the assessment right — and knowing when to escalate to urgent decompression — is a core registrar skill.

## Initial Assessment

When a referral comes in, your first job is to stratify: is this uncomplicated renal colic, or is there something that needs urgent intervention?

**History essentials:**
- Character of pain (classic loin-to-groin, colicky)
- Fever, rigors, or systemically unwell — infection changes everything
- Previous stones, stone type if known
- Single kidney or known renal anomaly
- Anticoagulation, bleeding risk
- Last void, any urinary symptoms

**Examination:**
- Temperature — this is your critical discriminator
- Loin tenderness (renal angle)
- Abdominal exam — exclude AAA in any older patient with flank pain
- Haemodynamic status

## Investigations

**Bloods:** FBC, U&E, CRP, clotting. Creatinine is your baseline for obstruction monitoring. Raised WCC + CRP + fever = infected obstructed system until proven otherwise.

**Urine dip:** Haematuria supports diagnosis but absence doesn't exclude it. Nitrites/leucocytes + fever = urgent.

**Imaging:**
- **CT KUB (non-contrast)** — gold standard. Identifies stone size, location, degree of hydronephrosis, alternative diagnoses. Request same-day or acutely if any clinical concern.
- **USS** — useful in pregnancy or to confirm hydronephrosis if CT not available acutely. Poor for distal ureteric stones.
- **Plain KUB** — largely superseded by CT; useful for follow-up of radio-opaque stones.

## Immediate Management

### Analgesia — priority one

| Drug | Dose | Route | Notes |
|------|------|-------|-------|
| Diclofenac | 75mg | IM or PR | First-line if no contraindications. Do not use in renal impairment (eGFR &lt;30), GI risk, or cardiovascular disease |
| Morphine | 5–10mg (titrate) | IV/IM | Effective. Combine with antiemetic. Use if NSAIDs contraindicated |
| Paracetamol | 1g | IV/PO | Useful adjunct; rarely sufficient alone for acute colic |

> Give analgesia first, then investigate. Do not make a patient with renal colic wait in pain for imaging.

**Antiemetics:** Cyclizine 50mg IV/IM or ondansetron 4mg IV — nausea is common and distressing.

**IV access + fluids:** Establish access. Gentle IV hydration if vomiting or can't tolerate oral. High-volume fluid loading does not accelerate stone passage and may worsen symptoms.

### Medical Expulsive Therapy (MET)

- **Tamsulosin 400 micrograms once daily** — alpha-blocker that relaxes ureteric smooth muscle
- Indicated for distal ureteric stones ≤10mm with a view to spontaneous passage
- Evidence base is mixed for stones &lt;5mm but commonly used in practice
- Counsel patient: postural hypotension risk, especially first dose

## Pro-Tips for the On-Call Registrar

- **The "CT-First" Reflex:** If the patient is not septic, prioritize the CT KUB. A formal stone size is the single most useful piece of information for deciding "Stent vs. Wait." Avoid relying on "guess-timation" from an old KUB.
- **Solitary Kidney Thresholds:** If the creatinine is rising in a patient with a stone in a solitary kidney, do not wait for the CT KUB if it's going to delay decompression. A quick USS to confirm hydronephrosis is enough to justify theatre.
- **NSAID-Watch:** FYs often struggle with NSAID contraindications. Always explicitly state "Check eGFR &gt; 30, no active GI bleed, no history of PUD." If they are on blood thinners, warn them: "We are trading a tiny GI bleed risk for a massive survival benefit for the stone."

## Indications for Urgent Decompression

This is the critical decision. **Urgent decompression (nephrostomy or retrograde stenting) is required if:**

1. **Infected obstructed kidney** — fever + obstruction = urosepsis until proven otherwise. This is a urological emergency. Call your consultant immediately. Sepsis 6, urgent imaging, and emergency decompression within hours.
2. **Bilateral obstruction** or **obstruction in a solitary/transplant kidney** with rising creatinine
3. **Uncontrollable pain** despite adequate analgesia
4. **Stone unlikely to pass spontaneously** (>10mm, proximal ureteric, impacted)
5. **Renal impairment** — rising creatinine in the context of obstruction

> If in doubt: call. An infected obstructed kidney can kill a patient within hours. This is not the time to watch and wait.

## Disposition

**Discharge criteria (uncomplicated, stone ≤5mm):**
- Afebrile, haemodynamically stable
- Pain controlled with oral analgesia
- Normal or baseline creatinine
- No infection markers
- Reliable patient with clear follow-up plan

**Prescribe on discharge:**
- Diclofenac 50mg TDS PRN (if appropriate) + PPI cover
- Tamsulosin 400mcg OD for MET (if distal stone ≤10mm)
- Urine filter (or strain at home) to catch stone for analysis
- Safety-net advice: return if fever, rigors, vomiting, or pain not controlled

**Outpatient follow-up:**
- Stone clinic or urology OPA within 2–4 weeks
- Repeat KUB or USS to confirm passage
- Metabolic stone workup if recurrent (24h urine, serum calcium, urate, bicarbonate)

## Quick Reference Summary

- Fever + obstruction = emergency. Do not discharge. Call consultant.
- CT KUB to confirm, size and locate stone
- NSAIDs first-line analgesia (if not contraindicated)
- Tamsulosin for distal ureteric stones ≤10mm
- Stones >10mm unlikely to pass — plan intervention
- Always confirm follow-up and give written safety-net advice
